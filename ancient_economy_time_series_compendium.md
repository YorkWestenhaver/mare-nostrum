# Compendium of Quantitative Time Series for the Ancient Economy

A reference catalogue of available proxy datasets for reconstructing the economic history of the Greco-Roman world, organized by measurement domain.

---

## I. Geochemical Proxies (Physical Measurements from Natural Archives)

These are the gold standard — continuous, independently dated, free of archaeological selection bias.

### 1. Greenland Ice Core Lead Pollution (NGRIP2)

- **What it measures:** Atmospheric lead deposition from silver-lead smelting — a direct proxy for monetary metal production and, by extension, aggregate economic activity in Europe and the Mediterranean.
- **Coverage:** 1100 BCE – 800 CE, sub-annual resolution, ±1–2 year dating uncertainty.
- **Key source:** McConnell, J.R. et al. (2018). "Lead pollution recorded in Greenland ice indicates European emissions tracked plagues, wars, and imperial expansion during antiquity." *PNAS* 115(22), 5726–5731.
- **Earlier/supporting data:** Hong et al. (1994) on GRIP core (18 discrete samples, 2-year averages — the original sparse dataset); Boutron & Patterson on Antarctic cores for Southern Hemisphere comparison.
- **Why it matters:** Only continuous, high-resolution, physically independent measure of aggregate European economic output. Shows emissions rising with Phoenician expansion, accelerating under Carthaginian/Roman Iberian mining, peaking under the early Empire, plunging with the Antonine plague (~165 CE), and staying depressed for 500+ years. Validates every other proxy by convergence.

### 2. Greenland Ice Core Copper Pollution (GRIP/NGRIP2)

- **What it measures:** Atmospheric copper from bronze smelting — independent of the lead/silver monetary signal.
- **Coverage:** ~2500 years ago to present, lower resolution than lead.
- **Key source:** Hong, S. et al. (1996). "History of Ancient Copper Smelting Pollution During Roman and Medieval Times Recorded in Greenland Ice." *Science* 272(5259), 246–249.
- **Why it matters:** Provides a non-monetary industrial production signal. Tracks a different metal and different set of economic activities than lead, but shows the same broad periodization.

### 3. Spanish/Scottish/Faroese Peat Bog Lead Deposition

- **What it measures:** Regional atmospheric lead pollution, same underlying signal as ice cores but with different geographic weighting (closer to Iberian mining sources).
- **Coverage:** Variable; some records span 4,600+ years.
- **Key source:** Martínez Cortizas, A. et al. (1999). "Mercury in a Spanish peat bog: Archive of climate change and atmospheric metal deposition." *Science* 284, 939–942; and related peat bog studies from Scotland and the Faroe Islands.
- **Why it matters:** Cross-validates the Greenland signal with independent archives from different locations, confirming that the pattern isn't an artifact of Arctic atmospheric transport modeling.

## II. Maritime Trade Proxies

### 4. Mediterranean Shipwreck Database (OXREP)

- **What it measures:** Volume of maritime trade, proxied by the count of known shipwrecks per period.
- **Coverage:** Earliest seafaring through 1500 CE; ~1,781 wrecks as of 2017 release.
- **Key sources:**
  - Parker, A.J. (1992). *Ancient Shipwrecks of the Mediterranean and the Roman Provinces.* BAR International Series 580. (Original catalogue, ~1,200 wrecks.)
  - Oxford Roman Economy Project (OXREP) Shipwrecks Database: https://oxrep.classics.ox.ac.uk/databases/shipwrecks_database/
  - Harvard DARMC (Digital Atlas of Roman and Medieval Civilizations) also integrates shipwreck data.
- **Known biases:** Discovery bias (modern diving activity concentrates in certain regions), preservation bias (amphora cargoes survive better than perishable goods), depth bias (Roman-era wrecks tend to be in shallower waters). Wilson and others have engaged these critiques; the signal is large enough to survive aggressive correction.
- **Why it matters:** The most "spectacular" time series (De Callataÿ's word) — shows an order-of-magnitude peak in 200 BCE–200 CE followed by collapse. Most contested but also most dramatic proxy for long-distance trade.

### 5. Amphora Distributions

- **What it measures:** Commodity flows of olive oil, wine, and fish sauce — directly traceable by amphora typology to specific production regions.
- **Coverage:** ~6th century BCE through late antiquity; best for 2nd century BCE – 5th century CE.
- **Key sources:**
  - Monte Testaccio (Rome): ~53 million Dressel 20 amphorae (Baetican olive oil) — Rodríguez Almeida, E. (1984). *Il Monte Testaccio.*
  - Dressel typology and Peacock & Williams (1986). *Amphorae and the Roman Economy.*
  - Various site-level quantification studies compiled by OXREP.
- **Why it matters:** Unlike shipwrecks (which count events), amphora distributions count goods. You can trace specific trade routes (Baetica → Rome for olive oil, Gaul → Britain for wine) and measure volume changes over time. The Baetican olive oil trade to Rome is one of the best-documented commodity flows in pre-modern history.

## III. Manufactured Goods / Industrial Proxies

### 6. Fine Ware Pottery Distributions (Terra Sigillata / African Red Slip)

- **What it measures:** Manufacturing output, trade networks, and consumer market access for non-subsistence goods.
- **Coverage:**
  - Italian terra sigillata (Arretine): ~1st century BCE – 1st century CE
  - Gaulish terra sigillata: ~1st – 3rd century CE
  - African Red Slip (ARS): ~mid-1st – 7th century CE
- **Key sources:**
  - Hayes, J.W. (1972). *Late Roman Pottery.* (Standard typology for ARS.)
  - Various site quantification studies; OXREP integrates much of this.
  - For earlier Greek: Attic black/red-figure distribution studies.
- **Why it matters:** Fine ware distribution tracks market integration and consumer prosperity. The sequential replacement of Italian → Gaulish → African production centers tells a story about shifting economic geography. ARS is particularly important for late antiquity — it shows the Eastern/North African Mediterranean maintaining exchange networks centuries after the West collapsed.

### 7. Fish-Salting Installations (Cetariae)

- **What it measures:** Industrial-scale food processing infrastructure — a proxy for capital investment and long-distance trade in preserved fish products (garum/liquamen).
- **Coverage:** Primarily 1st century BCE – 5th century CE; concentrated in Iberia, North Africa, Black Sea coast.
- **Key source:** Wilson, A. (2006). "The economic impact of technological advances in the Roman construction industry." In *Innovazione tecnica e progresso economico nel mondo romano*; and subsequent OXREP publications.
- **Why it matters:** These are large-scale industrial installations requiring significant capital. Their construction and abandonment dates provide a direct measure of productive capital stock in the food processing sector.

### 8. Water Mill Installations

- **What it measures:** Mechanized grain processing — a proxy for technological adoption and capital-intensive production.
- **Coverage:** Scattered evidence from 1st century BCE onward; major compilation by Wilson.
- **Key source:** Wilson, A. (2002). "Machines, Power and the Ancient Economy." *Journal of Roman Studies* 92, 1–32.
- **Why it matters:** Directly measures adoption of labor-saving technology, which bears on debates about Roman economic growth vs. stagnation.

### 9. Olive Oil and Wine Press Installations

- **What it measures:** Agricultural processing infrastructure — proxy for cash crop production scale.
- **Coverage:** Bronze Age through late antiquity; best data for Hellenistic and Roman periods.
- **Key source:** OXREP Presses Database: https://oxrep.classics.ox.ac.uk/databases/
- **Why it matters:** Olive oil and wine are high-value agricultural products requiring significant processing infrastructure. The count and scale of press installations over time tracks agricultural commercialization.

## IV. Construction and Resource Extraction

### 10. Dendrochronological Construction Timber Dataset

- **What it measures:** Construction activity, infrastructure investment, forest resource extraction, and long-distance timber trade.
- **Coverage:** 300 BCE – 700 CE; 20,397 absolutely dated archaeological woods from north of the Alps.
- **Key sources:**
  - Tegel, W. et al. (2025). "Woodlands of Antiquity: A millennium of dendrochronological data on forest exploitation and timber economy between the Alps and the Atlantic." *PNAS*.
  - Holstein (1980) — earlier dataset on construction timber quantities from Western Germany.
- **Why it matters:** High-resolution, absolutely dated proxy for construction booms and busts. Shows Roman occupation driving major increases in logging (including long-distance transport from primary forests), with a sharp tipping point in the 3rd century CE.

### 11. Roman Mines and Quarries Database

- **What it measures:** Extractive industry activity — mining for metals (gold, silver, copper, iron, lead) and quarrying for building stone and marble.
- **Coverage:** Republic through late antiquity; concentrated in Iberia, Britain, Dacia, Asia Minor, Egypt.
- **Key source:** OXREP Mines and Quarries Database: https://oxrep.classics.ox.ac.uk/databases/
- **Why it matters:** Direct evidence for industrial-scale resource extraction. Particularly important for understanding the fiscal/monetary economy (silver/gold mines) and monumental construction programs (marble quarries).

## V. Biological / Dietary Proxies

### 12. Animal Bone Assemblages (Zooarchaeological Data)

- **What it measures:** Diet composition, meat consumption levels, and agricultural surplus — high income-elasticity goods that proxy for real income above subsistence.
- **Coverage:** Late Iron Age through early medieval; hundreds of site assemblages across the Empire.
- **Key sources:**
  - King, A. (1999). "Diet in the Roman world: a regional inter-site comparison of the mammal bones." *Journal of Roman Archaeology* 12, 168–202.
  - Jongman, W. (2007). "Gibbon was right: the decline and fall of the Roman economy." In *Crises and the Roman Empire.*
  - Bakels, C.C. & Jacomet, S. (2003). "Access to luxury foods in Central Europe during the Roman period." *World Archaeology* 34, 542–557.
- **Why it matters:** Meat consumption is one of the strongest proxies for living standards above subsistence. The assemblages show a clear rise-and-fall pattern matching the other economic proxies.

### 13. Cattle Biometry (Livestock Size Data)

- **What it measures:** Animal husbandry intensification — larger cattle indicate selective breeding, improved fodder, and organized livestock trade.
- **Coverage:** Late Iron Age through post-Roman; particularly strong for the Rhine frontier region.
- **Key sources:**
  - Trentacoste, A. et al. (2022). "Innovation and Intensification: The Use of Cattle in the Roman Rhine Region." *European Journal of Archaeology.*
  - Data compiled using log-ratio (LSI) technique across 606,000+ animal remains and ~15,000 cattle bone measurements.
- **Why it matters:** Livestock size directly reflects agricultural sophistication and capital investment in animal husbandry. Size increases with Romanization and collapses post-Roman — one of the clearest "Romanization = economic development" signals.

### 14. Human Skeletal Height Data

- **What it measures:** Biological standard of living — childhood nutritional adequacy, disease burden, and overall population health.
- **Coverage:** Scattered across the ancient world; best compilations for the Roman period by Jongman and Koepke/Baten.
- **Key sources:**
  - Koepke, N. & Baten, J. (2005). "The biological standard of living in Europe during the last two millennia." *European Review of Economic History* 9, 61–95.
  - Jongman, W. (2007) — integrates height data with other proxy evidence.
- **Why it matters:** Direct measure of population welfare that doesn't depend on monetary or trade data. Shows the same inverted-U as economic proxies — improvement through early Empire, decline from the late 2nd century.

## VI. Agricultural Land Use and Environment

### 15. Pollen Core Records (Palynology)

- **What it measures:** Agricultural vs. forest land cover — cereal pollen, olive pollen, pastoral indicators, and deforestation signals.
- **Coverage:** Continuous records spanning millennia, from lake/bog sediments across the Mediterranean and Northern Europe.
- **Key sources:**
  - Izdebski, A. et al. (2020). "Landscape Change and Trade in Ancient Greece: Evidence from Pollen Data."
  - Various regional pollen studies compiled for the Mediterranean, Balkans, Near East.
  - SEAD (Strategic Environmental Archaeology Database) aggregates some pollen data.
- **Why it matters:** Independently dated, continuous, and not subject to archaeological recovery bias. Tracks agricultural extensification (forest clearance for farming) and intensification (cash crop pollen). Regional resolution allows comparison across the Empire.

### 16. Paleoclimate Reconstructions (Dendroclimatology and Speleothems)

- **What it measures:** Temperature and precipitation variability — exogenous shocks to agricultural output.
- **Coverage:** Continuous; tree-ring chronologies extend back millennia in some regions; speleothems (cave formations) provide even longer records.
- **Key sources:**
  - Büntgen, U. et al. (2011). "2500 years of European climate variability and human susceptibility." *Science* 331, 578–582. (Alpine tree-ring reconstruction.)
  - McCormick, M. et al. (2012). "Climate change during and after the Roman Empire: Reconstructing the past from scientific and historical evidence." *Journal of Interdisciplinary History* 43(2), 169–220.
  - PAGES 2k Consortium datasets for multi-proxy temperature reconstructions.
- **Why it matters:** The "Roman Climate Optimum" (roughly 200 BCE – 200 CE) correlates with peak economic performance. Understanding climate as an exogenous variable is essential for causal analysis — it helps distinguish between endogenous economic dynamics and environmental forcing.

## VII. Monetary and Fiscal Proxies

### 17. Coin Hoard Frequency Data

- **What it measures:** Frequency of unrecovered coin hoards per period — a proxy for political instability, population decline, and economic disruption (hoards that aren't recovered imply the owner died or fled).
- **Coverage:** Republic through late antiquity; compiled from published hoard records across the Empire.
- **Key source:** Turchin, P. & Scheidel, W. (2009). "Coin Hoards Speak of Population Decline in Ancient Rome." *PNAS* 106(41), 17276–17279.
- **Why it matters:** Counterintuitive but powerful — the rate of non-recovery is the signal. Spikes in hoarding correlate with the Antonine plague, 3rd-century crisis, and other periods of demographic/political collapse.

### 18. Denarius Debasement Series

- **What it measures:** Silver content of Roman coinage over time — a direct measure of fiscal policy, inflation, and state financial stress.
- **Coverage:** ~211 BCE (introduction of the denarius) through the 3rd-century collapse and Diocletianic reforms.
- **Key source:** Harl, K.W. (1996). *Coinage in the Roman Economy, 300 B.C. to A.D. 700.* Johns Hopkins University Press. Also Duncan-Jones, R. (1994). *Money and Government in the Roman Empire.*
- **Why it matters:** One of the most precisely measurable time series in ancient history — you can assay the metal content of surviving coins with modern techniques. The progressive debasement from ~95% silver under Augustus to ~5% by the 260s CE is a direct fiscal-monetary signal.

### 19. Egyptian Papyrus Price and Wage Data

- **What it measures:** Commodity prices, wages, land rents, and tax payments — the closest thing to actual economic statistics in the ancient world.
- **Coverage:** Ptolemaic through Byzantine Egypt; best data for 1st – 3rd centuries CE, with a second cluster in the late 3rd – 4th centuries (post-Diocletianic).
- **Key sources:**
  - Rathbone, D. (1991). *Economic Rationalism and Rural Society in Third-Century A.D. Egypt.* (Heroninos archive — a single large estate's records.)
  - Rathbone, D. (1997). "Prices and price formation in Roman Egypt." In *Prix et formation des prix dans les économies antiques.*
  - Bagnall, R. (1985). *Currency and Inflation in Fourth Century Egypt.*
  - Drexhage, H.-J. (1991). *Preise, Mieten/Pachten, Kosten und Löhne im römischen Ägypten.*
- **Why it matters:** The only region with anything approaching continuous price/wage data. Egypt's unusual papyrus preservation gives us thousands of economic documents. The severe limitation is that Egypt may not be representative of the wider Empire.

### 20. Diocletian's Price Edict (301 CE)

- **What it measures:** Maximum prices for ~1,300 goods and services — a cross-sectional snapshot of relative prices and wages across the Empire.
- **Coverage:** Single point in time (301 CE), but covers the entire Empire in principle.
- **Key source:** Lauffer, S. (1971). *Diokletians Preisedikt.* More recently, Allen, R.C. (2009). "How prosperous were the Romans?"
- **Why it matters:** The single most comprehensive price list from the ancient world. Useful for relative price structures and cross-sector wage comparisons, even though the edict's prices may not reflect actual market prices.

## VIII. Epigraphic and Textual Proxies

### 21. Latin/Greek Inscription Counts ("The Epigraphic Habit")

- **What it measures:** Volume of inscriptions commissioned per period — a proxy for economic surplus, literacy, civic investment, and cultural vitality.
- **Coverage:** Republic through late antiquity; hundreds of thousands of inscriptions catalogued.
- **Key sources:**
  - Corpus Inscriptionum Latinarum (CIL) — the master corpus of Latin inscriptions.
  - Epigraphic Database Heidelberg (EDH): https://edh.ub.uni-heidelberg.de/
  - Clauss-Slaby Epigraphic Database (EDCS): http://www.manfredclauss.de/
  - MacMullen, R. (1982). "The Epigraphic Habit in the Roman Empire." *American Journal of Philology* 103, 233–246.
- **Why it matters:** Inscription commissioning costs money and reflects disposable surplus. The peak in the 2nd century CE and collapse in the 3rd mirrors every other economic proxy. Also provides qualitative information (occupational data, trade references, price mentions) embedded in individual inscriptions.

### 22. Occupational Inscriptions and Collegium Data

- **What it measures:** Economic specialization, division of labor, and trade organization.
- **Coverage:** Primarily 1st – 3rd centuries CE; concentrated in Italy, Gaul, Asia Minor.
- **Key sources:**
  - Waltzing, J.-P. (1895–1900). *Étude historique sur les corporations professionnelles chez les Romains.* (4 vols. — still foundational.)
  - Van Nijf, O.M. (1997). *The Civic World of Professional Associations in the Roman East.*
  - Joshel, S.R. (1992). *Work, Identity, and Legal Status at Rome.*
- **Why it matters:** The variety and specialization of occupations attested in inscriptions is a direct measure of economic complexity and division of labor. Dozens of distinct occupations attested in a single city implies deep market specialization.

## IX. Settlement and Demographic Proxies

### 23. Archaeological Field Survey Data (Settlement Counts)

- **What it measures:** Population density, settlement patterns, and rural site occupation over time.
- **Coverage:** Variable by region; major surveys exist for the Tiber Valley, North Africa (UNESCO Libyan Valleys Survey), Greece (various), Levant, Iberia.
- **Key sources:**
  - Bowman, A. & Wilson, A. (eds.) (2011). *Settlement, Urbanization, and Population.* Oxford Studies on the Roman Economy.
  - Various regional surveys compiled and compared in OXREP publications.
  - DARMC (Digital Atlas of Roman and Medieval Civilizations) at Harvard.
- **Why it matters:** Systematic surveys of defined areas avoid the selection bias of excavation-focused archaeology. Counting sites per period gives population density estimates with regional resolution. Shows urbanization expanding through the Republic/early Empire and contracting in late antiquity, with critical East-West divergence.

### 24. Population Estimates

- **What it measures:** Total and regional population — the denominator for any per-capita calculation.
- **Coverage:** Point estimates for various dates; debates center on 1st – 2nd century CE peak.
- **Key sources:**
  - Scheidel, W. (2007). "Demography." In *The Cambridge Economic History of the Greco-Roman World,* 38–86.
  - Frier, B.W. (2000). "The demography of the Early Roman Empire." In *CAH* XI, 2nd ed.
  - Maddison, A. (2007). *Contours of the World Economy, 1–2030 AD.* (Table 1.3 for regional breakdown.)
  - Lo Cascio, E. — various publications arguing for higher Italian/imperial population counts.
- **Why it matters:** All per-capita calculations depend on population estimates, and these remain deeply contested (60–70+ million for the Empire at peak). The "high count" vs. "low count" debate for Roman Italy alone changes GDP per capita estimates significantly.

## X. Derived / Synthetic Estimates

### 25. GDP Estimates (Point Estimates, Not True Time Series)

- **What it measures:** Aggregate and per-capita economic output — the ultimate derived quantity.
- **Coverage:** Point estimates, mostly for the early Empire (~1–2nd century CE).
- **Key sources, in chronological order of scholarship:**
  - Goldsmith, R.W. (1984). "An Estimate of the Size and Structure of the National Product of the Early Roman Empire." *Review of Income and Wealth* 30(3), 263–288. (Pioneer.)
  - Temin, P. (2006). "Estimating GDP in the Early Roman Empire." In *Innovazione tecnica e progresso economico nel mondo romano.*
  - Maddison, A. (2007). *Contours of the World Economy, 1–2030 AD.*
  - Scheidel, W. & Friesen, S.J. (2009). "The Size of the Economy and the Distribution of Income in the Roman Empire." *JRS* 99, 61–91.
  - Lo Cascio, E. & Malanima, P. (2009). "Ancient and pre-modern economies: GDP in the Roman Empire and early modern Europe." In *Quantifying the Greco-Roman Economy.*
  - Maddison Project Database (2023 release) — current standard for cross-historical comparison.
- **Estimates range:** ~$400–$1,400 per capita in 2011 PPP dollars depending on assumptions. Scheidel-Friesen: ~300–380 sesterces mean per capita; ~707 kg wheat-equivalent mean income.
- **Why it matters:** These are the numbers people cite, but they are entirely derived from the proxies listed above. Every input is uncertain by wide margins. Useful for order-of-magnitude cross-civilization comparison (Rome vs. Han China vs. medieval Europe) but should never be mistaken for measured data.

### 26. Income Distribution Estimates (Gini Coefficients)

- **What it measures:** Inequality within the Roman economy.
- **Coverage:** Point estimates for the early Empire and selected Hellenistic polities.
- **Key sources:**
  - Scheidel, W. & Friesen, S.J. (2009) — income distribution across 13 classes for the Roman Empire.
  - Milanovic, B., Lindert, P.H., & Williamson, J.G. (2011). "Pre-Industrial Inequality." *Economic Journal* 121, 255–272.
  - Kron, G. (2014) — Gini estimates for individual Greco-Roman cities (Ampurias, Carthage, Pergamum, Priene, Olynthus).
  - Delos property inscription studies — probabilistic inequality estimates from epigraphic data.
- **Why it matters:** Understanding the distribution matters as much as the aggregate. High inequality with a small "middle class" (Scheidel's position) vs. a more graduated distribution (Lo Cascio) has major implications for interpreting consumption proxies.

## Key Integrative References

These works synthesize multiple proxy datasets and are the best starting points for understanding the field:

- **Scheidel, W., Morris, I., & Saller, R.P. (eds.) (2007).** *The Cambridge Economic History of the Greco-Roman World.* Cambridge University Press. — The standard comprehensive reference.
- **Bowman, A. & Wilson, A. (eds.) (2009–2018).** Oxford Studies on the Roman Economy series (multiple volumes on quantification, settlement, urbanization, trade). — The OXREP output; most ambitious compilation of archaeological proxy data.
- **De Callataÿ, F. (2005).** "The Graeco-Roman Economy in the Super Long-Run: Lead, Copper, and Shipwrecks." *Journal of Roman Archaeology* 18, 361–372. — The paper that first systematically aligned the three "super long-run" proxies.
- **Jongman, W. (2007).** "Gibbon was Right: The Decline and Fall of the Roman Economy." — Masterful integration of animal bones, skeletal height, ice cores, and other proxies to argue for real economic decline from the late 2nd century.
- **Wilson, A. (2009).** "Quantifying Roman economic performance by means of proxies: pitfalls and potential." In *Quantifying the Greco-Roman Economy and Beyond.* — The most clear-eyed methodological critique of proxy usage, from someone who builds the datasets.
- **Ward-Perkins, B. (2005).** *The Fall of Rome and the End of Civilization.* Oxford University Press. — Uses pottery, building complexity, coinage, and other material evidence to argue for catastrophic economic decline in the West. Directly relevant to the anti-Pirenne case.
