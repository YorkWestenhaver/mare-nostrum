# What the Data Shows: A Plain-Language Narrative

## 1. What the Data Shows

The data reveals a clear arc of Mediterranean economic activity from the late 4th century BCE through the early medieval period. Lead emissions—a proxy for mining and metallurgy—rise from a low baseline in the mid-4th century BCE, reach an imperial peak around the turn of the Common Era, then undergo a sharp collapse in the mid-2nd century CE. That collapse is the largest structural break in the entire series. Emissions remain low for roughly five centuries before recovering in the late 7th century and rising again to a medieval high in the mid-8th century.

Shipwreck counts—a proxy for maritime trade intensity—follow a related but distinct trajectory. Mediterranean-wide breaks occur around 375 BCE, 125 CE, and 375 CE. Critically, the Western and Eastern Mediterranean diverge: the Western basin drops below 10% of its peak trade volume by 475 CE, while the Eastern basin remains near its peak until much later, falling below 10% only around 775 CE. The ratio of Western to Eastern trade reverses dramatically—from roughly 8:1 at the Roman peak to roughly 1:2 in the pre-Islamic period (500–650 CE). By 575 CE, the Eastern Mediterranean is still at 91% of its peak, while the Western Mediterranean has fallen to 4%.

Lead emissions and shipwreck counts are weakly correlated (r ≈ 0.20). They measure different aspects of economic activity: one reflects mining and industry, the other maritime commerce. Both show structural breaks, but they do not move in lockstep.

## 3. Regional Divergence and Its Significance

The most striking finding is the **asynchronous decline** of the Western and Eastern Mediterranean. The Western basin collapses first: by 475 CE it has dropped below 10% of its peak. The Eastern basin, by contrast, remains near its peak until the mid-6th century and does not fall below 10% until around 775 CE.

This reversal of the Western-to-Eastern ratio—from 8.2 at the Roman peak to 0.5 in the pre-Islamic period—indicates that the center of Mediterranean trade shifted eastward well before the Islamic conquests. The Eastern Mediterranean, under Byzantine control, sustained high trade volumes through the 6th century, while the Western Empire had already fragmented and its maritime commerce had collapsed.

North African–connected shipwrecks tell a parallel story: 29 wrecks in the pre-Vandal period, 4 during the Vandal period (429–439 CE), 2 during the Byzantine reconquest (533–534 CE), and none in the Islamic period. The data suggests a sharp contraction of North African maritime connectivity after the Vandal conquest and little recovery under Byzantine or Islamic rule.

---

## 4. Key Finding: Timing of Decline

**The Western Mediterranean’s trade collapse precedes the Islamic conquests by roughly two centuries.** The Western basin drops below 10% of peak by 475 CE—one year before the conventional date for the fall of the Western Roman Empire. The Eastern basin, by contrast, remains robust through the Plague of Justinian (541–542 CE) and the early Islamic conquests, declining below 10% of peak only around 775 CE.

The lead emissions series reinforces this picture: the largest structural break occurs around 174 CE, in the wake of the Antonine Plague. A second sharp decline in silver content of the denarius occurs between 253–268 CE (Gallienus), at roughly −2.9% per year, coinciding with the Plague of Cyprian and the Crisis of the Third Century. The data thus points to **plague and political crisis in the 2nd–3rd centuries** as major drivers of early decline, and to **Western collapse in the 5th century** as a distinct, pre-Islamic phenomenon.

## 5. Caveats

**Barrel bias:** Shipwreck counts reflect vessels that carried amphorae or other datable cargo. Ships carrying bulk goods in barrels or sacks leave fewer datable traces and may be undercounted. Shifts in packaging technology could distort the record.

**Discovery bias:** Wrecks are more likely to be found in heavily surveyed waters (e.g., Western Mediterranean coastlines) and in shallow waters. Deep-water or Eastern Mediterranean wrecks may be systematically underreported. Discovery rates also vary with modern economic activity (diving, dredging, construction).

**Lead emissions:** Estimates depend on ice-core or peat records from specific sites. Regional variation in atmospheric circulation may mean that one or a few cores do not perfectly represent Mediterranean-wide activity.

**Correlation ≠ causation:** Change-points align with known historical events but do not prove that those events caused the shifts. Multiple factors—plague, war, fiscal collapse, institutional change—likely interacted.
