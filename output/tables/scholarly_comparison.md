## Detailed Analysis

### Pirenne (1937)

Pirenne argued that the Islamic conquests of the 7th century closed the western Mediterranean to long-distance trade, cutting off the Carolingian world from the Mediterranean and precipitating feudalism. Our data contradicts the causal sequence: the Western Mediterranean had already collapsed by 475 CE, well before the rise of Islam. The Eastern Mediterranean, which came under Islamic control, remained the dominant trade basin and did not drop below 10% of peak until 775 CE. If Islam "closed" anything, it was the East—and that decline came later. The Western collapse appears to be a consequence of the fall of the Western Roman Empire and its fiscal and institutional structures, not of Islamic expansion.

### Ward-Perkins (2005)

Ward-Perkins emphasized real, material decline beginning in the 3rd–5th centuries, before Islam. The data strongly supports this. The largest structural break in lead emissions occurs at 174 CE, in the wake of the Antonine Plague. The steepest denarius debasement (253–268 CE) coincides with the Plague of Cyprian and the Crisis of the Third Century. Western Mediterranean shipwreck counts fall below 10% of peak by 475 CE. The decline is measurable, gradual in the sense of unfolding over centuries, and clearly pre-Islamic.

### McCormick (2001)

McCormick argued that Mediterranean trade did not simply die but transformed—new routes, new goods, new patterns. The weak correlation between lead emissions and shipwreck counts (r=0.20) supports the idea that different economic activities followed different trajectories. The Eastern Mediterranean's sustained high trade volume through the 6th century suggests continuity and transformation rather than uniform collapse. However, the Western Mediterranean at 4% of peak by 575 CE indicates that "transformation" in the West looked more like collapse. The verdict is partial: transformation in the East, collapse in the West.

### Harper (2017)

Harper identified plagues as key drivers of Roman economic decline. The data aligns well: the lead emissions crash at 174 CE follows the Antonine Plague (165–193 CE); the steepest denarius decline (253–268 CE) coincides with the Plague of Cyprian (249–270 CE); and the Eastern Mediterranean's eventual decline occurs after the Plague of Justinian (541–542 CE). The timing of structural breaks and plague events is consistent with plague as a major economic shock. The data does not prove causation but strongly supports the correlation Harper emphasizes.

### Wickham (2005)

Wickham argued that Roman economic complexity was historically unique and that its simplification was a structural process, not merely a political or military event. The data supports the reality of structural simplification: clear change-points, regional divergence, and measurable decline in both lead emissions and shipwreck counts. The modification is that simplification was regionally uneven. The Eastern Mediterranean retained complexity longer than the West; the Western-to-Eastern ratio reversed from 8.2 to 0.5. Wickham's emphasis on structural change is supported, but the data adds nuance: simplification was asynchronous and region-specific.
