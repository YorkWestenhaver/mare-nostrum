The Question

What killed Mediterranean trade?

For nearly a century, historians have debated the economic collapse of the ancient Mediterranean world. Was it the Germanic migrations? The rise of Islam? Plagues? A slow structural unwinding? Each scholar has a theory — and each cites different evidence.

This project takes a different approach: start from the numbers, find the structural breaks, then see which story the data supports. No priors. No thesis to defend. Just change-point detection on fourteen independent quantitative proxies that span 2,000 years — from the Roman Republic to the Carolingian revival.

Proxy 1 — Industrial Activity

Lead pollution: a 1,400-year record of mining

Lead isotopes trapped in Greenland ice record the rise and fall of European metal smelting with annual precision. The signal is unambiguous: a massive industrial expansion under the Roman Republic and early Empire, then a catastrophic crash around 174 CE — the single largest structural break in the entire 1,400-year record.

Annual European lead emissions estimated from NGRIP2 ice core (McConnell et al. 2018). Colored bands show segments identified by blind change-point detection (PELT algorithm). Hover over the chart for values.

The 174 CE crash is the largest break in the record. Emissions drop from 0.91 to 0.33 kt/year — a 64% decline — coincident with the Antonine Plague (165–193 CE). Industrial mining activity doesn't recover for 500 years.
Zoom In

The Augustan peak: why lead spiked around 5 CE

The single highest point in the early record — ~1.35 kt/yr around 5–10 CE — demands explanation. After the Cantabrian Wars (~29–19 BCE), Augustus consolidated control of the Iberian Peninsula, unlocking industrial-scale exploitation of the silver-lead mines at Rio Tinto, Cartagena, and Las Médulas. For a brief window, Roman industrial output approached levels not seen again until the early modern period.

Zoomed view of lead emissions from 100 BCE to 200 CE. The Augustan peak is clearly visible, driven by Iberian silver mining. A recent study (Needleman et al. 2024, PNAS) estimated this pollution raised blood lead levels by ~2.4 μg/dL across Europe.

Proxy 2 — Maritime Trade

Shipwrecks: counting the vessels that never arrived

The number of shipwrecks per period is a direct proxy for the volume of maritime trade. More ships meant more sinkings. The Oxford Roman Economy Project catalogues 1,784 wrecks across the Mediterranean, dated using equal-probability methods to avoid midpoint distortion.

Mediterranean shipwreck density in 25-year bins with equal-probability dating (Wilson 2011 method). Regional totals stacked. Hover for breakdown by region.

Trade peaks around 25–75 CE, then begins a two-stage decline: a moderate drop after 125 CE and a steep collapse after 375 CE. But the critical story is in the regional breakdown…
The Key Discovery

Two Mediterraneans: a tale of East and West

When you split the shipwreck data by region, the most important finding emerges. The Western and Eastern Mediterranean didn't decline together — the West collapsed first, by at least 250 years.

Western vs. Eastern Mediterranean shipwreck counts (50-year bins). The West drops below 10% of peak by 475 CE; the East sustains near-peak trade until ~625 CE.

Part III — Fiscal Collapse & Monetary History
The Money

Three currencies, two civilizations, two collapses

Rome ran a bimetallic system: the silver denarius for everyday transactions, the gold aureus for large payments and state finance. When the silver currency collapsed, Constantine replaced the aureus with the solidus — which then held stable for an astonishing 500 years. Meanwhile, the Islamic dirham replaced Roman silver in the East.

Silver: the denarius death spiral

Silver content of the Roman denarius, 210 BCE to 294 CE (Butcher & Ponting 2015; Walker 1976). Each point = one emperor or period.

Gold: from aureus to solidus

While silver was debased ruthlessly, gold told a different story. The aureus lost weight gradually but maintained purity much longer. Constantine's solidus reset the gold standard at 4.5g — and it held at ~98% purity from 309 CE until the 11th century, when Byzantine fiscal crises finally broke it.

Gold coin weight over time: Roman aureus (46 BCE–309 CE) and Byzantine solidus (309–1092 CE). Purity stayed above 95% for most of this period; the collapse came in weight, then finally in purity under the Comneni.

The other Mediterranean: the Islamic dirham

After 696 CE, the Umayyad silver dirham became the dominant currency from Morocco to Central Asia. Lead isotope analysis traces its silver to mines spanning three continents. Plotted alongside the denarius and solidus, it shows how monetary leadership shifted from Rome to Constantinople to Baghdad.

Islamic silver dirham weight standard, 696–940 CE. The Umayyad reform established a stable ~2.97g standard; Abbasid-era variation reflects the vast geographic scope of silver sourcing.

The denarius lost 95% of its silver in under two centuries. The solidus held steady for 500 years. The dirham introduced a new silver standard for the Islamic world. Three currencies, three stories of state capacity.
Part IV — The Evidence Converges
Prices

Egyptian wheat: the only ancient price series

Thousands of papyrus documents from Roman Egypt record wheat prices in artabas. This is the only usable long-run price series from anywhere in the Roman Empire. It shows remarkable price stability for over a century — then the Antonine Plague shattered it.

Egyptian wheat prices in drachmas per artaba (Rathbone & Temin). Stable at ~8 dr/artaba for a century, then doubling after 160 CE. Hyperinflation follows in the late 3rd century.

Prices doubled between 160–190 CE — the same window as the lead crash and the Antonine Plague. The only ancient price series confirms what the ice cores show: the 2nd century was the turning point.
Fear Index

Coin hoards: burying your savings and never coming back

When people bury coins, it means they fear for the future. When they never return to dig them up, it means the worst happened. The CHRE database catalogues over 18,000 hoards. Plotting their frequency by terminal date creates a "fear index" for the ancient world.

Coin hoard frequency by 25-year period (terminal date). Spikes correspond to civil wars, invasions, and plagues. Data from the Coin Hoards of the Roman Empire project (Oxford).

The massive 250–275 CE spike is unmistakable — the Crisis of the Third Century, when the Empire nearly disintegrated. Hoarding peaks align perfectly with the denarius collapse and the Plague of Cyprian.
The Denominator

Population: the number that explains everything else

All other indicators — trade volume, mining output, tax revenue — depend on how many people were alive. Roman population estimates are uncertain, but scholars broadly agree on the trajectory: growth through the 1st century, catastrophic loss from the Antonine Plague, and never a full recovery.

Estimated population of the Roman Empire (Scheidel 2006; Frier 2000). Shaded band shows scholarly uncertainty range. The Antonine Plague (~165 CE) and Plague of Cyprian (~250 CE) are both visible.

An estimated 20–30% of the population died in the Antonine Plague. When you lose a quarter of your people, everything else follows: tax revenues fall, mines close, trade contracts, the army can't recruit. Population is the denominator.
Part V — Why Did It Happen?
Climate

536 CE: the worst year to be alive

In 536 CE, a massive volcanic eruption (probably in Iceland) threw enough sulfur into the stratosphere to dim the sun across Europe for 18 months. Temperatures plunged. Crops failed from Ireland to China. A second eruption in 540 CE compounded the disaster. Together they triggered the Late Antique Little Ice Age (~536–660 CE), which coincided with the Plague of Justinian and the final collapse of Mediterranean trade networks.

Top: Summer temperature anomaly from Central European tree rings (Büntgen et al. 2011, sampled at 5-year intervals). Bottom: Major volcanic eruptions (eVolv2k v3, sulfur injection in Tg SO₂). The 536/540 CE double eruption is the most dramatic climate event in the record.

Tree rings show the 536 CE event as the sharpest cooling in 2,500 years. Danish oaks lost 53% of their growth by 539 CE. The climate shock provides a causal mechanism linking volcanic forcing → crop failure → famine → plague susceptibility → economic collapse.
Biology

The forests grew back: pollen as a collapse indicator

When farms are abandoned and mines close, forests return. Palynological evidence from nearly 1,000 pollen cores across Europe reveals a dramatic surge in tree pollen between 250 and 550 CE — forests literally reclaimed Roman farmland. The trend reverses around 800–1000 CE with Carolingian-era land clearance.

Percentage of arboreal (tree) pollen in European sediment cores (Fyfe et al. 2015, PANGAEA). Higher values = more forest, less farmland. 200-year bins, 982 records across NW Europe.

Forest regrowth is the biological mirror of lead pollution decline. When the mines closed and farms were abandoned, the trees returned. The pollen record independently confirms the economic collapse visible in every other dataset.
Part VI — The Recovery
Building Again

Churches: the medieval recovery signal

After centuries of economic contraction, Europe began building again. The construction of major churches and cathedrals is one of the clearest quantitative signals of renewed economic capacity. The pattern is stark: near-zero monumental construction from 700–950 CE, then a dramatic acceleration into the "Age of the Cathedrals."

Major church construction starts per 25-year period across Western Europe (Rijpma dataset, ~1,695 churches in Italy, France, Germany, Britain, Low Countries). The takeoff begins ~975 CE.

The church construction boom begins exactly when lead emissions surge. Around 750–800 CE, Carolingian and Merovingian mines reopened across France and Germany. Construction follows within a generation. The recovery was real and measurable.
Monetization

Britain's lost coins: the Anglo-Saxon gap

The Portable Antiquities Scheme has recorded over 1.7 million archaeological finds across Britain. The coin loss data tells an extraordinary story: Roman Britain was intensely monetized — coins everywhere. After ~410 CE, coin use virtually vanished. Recovery came slowly, with silver sceattas from ~680 CE and then the penny.

Coin finds per period from the Portable Antiquities Scheme database (Britain). The "Anglo-Saxon gap" (~410–680 CE) represents near-total de-monetization of the British economy.

For roughly 270 years, Britain barely used coins at all. The transition from a fully monetized Roman province to a near-barter economy is one of the most dramatic economic reversals in European history — and the recovery mirrors the church construction and lead emission patterns.
Synthesis

All proxies, overlaid

When we normalize each dataset to its peak value and plot them together, the convergence is striking. Lead, shipwrecks, and the denarius all agree on the general arc: a peak in the 1st century CE, a sharp decline beginning in the 2nd–3rd century, and a deep trough lasting centuries.

But they measure different things and decline at different rates. Lead crashes first (174 CE). The denarius follows (180–268 CE). Shipwrecks drop last and most gradually, with the Eastern Mediterranean holding on far longer than the West. The new datasets — wheat prices, coin hoards, tree rings — all confirm the same inflection points.

All three proxies normalized to their respective peak values (100%). Historical events annotated. The weak cross-correlation (Pearson r = 0.20) confirms these measure genuinely different economic dimensions.

Methodology

Blind change-point detection

Every structural break shown in this analysis was detected algorithmically, using binary segmentation with an RBF kernel (ruptures library). No historical dates were supplied as priors. The algorithm simply looked for the points where the statistical properties of the data changed most dramatically.

All detected change-points across proxies and regions. Each vertical line represents a blind structural break. They cluster around known historical crises — but the algorithm didn't know that.

Antonine Plague (165 CE) Plague of Cyprian (249 CE) Plague of Justinian (541 CE) Fall of Western Empire (476 CE) Islamic Conquests (630s CE) Merovingian Mines Reopen (~750 CE)
The Debate

Five historians, five theories — what does the data say?

Each of these scholars proposed a different explanation for the collapse of Mediterranean trade. We compared their claims against the quantitative evidence, line by line. Click each card to read the detailed verdict.

Not Supported
Henri Pirenne (1937)
"The Islamic conquest closed Western Mediterranean trade, causing Carolingian feudalism."
The Western Mediterranean drops below 10% of peak at 475 CE — two full centuries before the Islamic conquests of the 630s–640s. The Eastern Mediterranean, which actually came under Islamic control, sustained high trade through the 6th century. The causal sequence Pirenne proposed is reversed: the West collapsed long before Islam arrived.
Strongly Supported
Bryan Ward-Perkins (2005)
"There was a real, material decline — gradual and pre-Islamic."
Lead emissions crash at 174 CE (post–Antonine Plague). The denarius steepest decline is 253–268 CE (Plague of Cyprian, Crisis of the Third Century). Western Med below 10% of peak by 475 CE. The decline is measurable, material, and unambiguously pre-Islamic.
Partially Supported
Michael McCormick (2001)
"Trade didn't die — it transformed. New routes, new goods."
The weak lead–shipwreck correlation (r = 0.20) supports the idea that different economic activities diverged. The Eastern Mediterranean's sustained trade through the 6th century suggests transformation and continuity. But the Western Med at 4% of peak by 575 CE looks more like collapse than transformation.
Strongly Supported
Kyle Harper (2017)
"Plagues were key drivers of economic decline."
Lead crash at 174 CE follows the Antonine Plague. Denarius decline at 253–268 CE coincides with the Plague of Cyprian. The timing of every major structural break aligns with a known pandemic event. Plague as economic shock is strongly consistent with the data.
Modified Support
Chris Wickham (2005)
"Roman complexity was unique; simplification was structural and inevitable."
Structural simplification is real and measurable. But it was regionally uneven: the East retained complexity for 250 years longer than the West. The West-to-East ratio reverses from 8:1 to 1:2. Simplification was asynchronous, not a single event.
Caveats

What the data can't tell us

  • Barrel bias. After the 2nd century, a shift from amphorae to barrels means late-period trade may be systematically undercounted in the shipwreck record. Ships carrying barrels leave fewer datable traces.
  • Discovery bias. More wrecks have been found in French and Italian waters; the North African coast is under-surveyed. This may overweight Western Mediterranean data in earlier periods.
  • Lead proxy scope. The ice-core record reflects primarily Iberian and northern European mining, weighted by atmospheric transport to Greenland. It may not fully represent Eastern Mediterranean industrial activity.
  • Correlation is not causation. Change-points align with known events but don't prove those events caused the shifts. Plague, war, fiscal crisis, and institutional decay likely interacted.
  • Pollen resolution. The pollen data uses 200-year bins, far coarser than the other proxies. It captures broad trends but cannot pinpoint the timing of specific events.
  • PAS geographic bias. The Portable Antiquities Scheme is Britain-only. Monetization patterns in Gaul, Iberia, or the Eastern Mediterranean may have differed significantly.
  • Population estimates. Ancient population figures are among the most contested numbers in Roman history. The "high count" and "low count" schools differ by nearly 2x. We show the mid-range consensus.
  • Compiled numismatic data. The aureus, solidus, and dirham datasets are compiled from multiple academic sources. Individual coins varied; the values shown represent scholarly averages for each period.
Methodology Details

Equal-probability dating: Shipwrecks with date ranges contribute fractional counts to each year in the range, avoiding midpoint distortion (Wilson 2011).

Change-point detection: Binary segmentation with RBF kernel (ruptures library), no historical dates as priors. Penalty parameter selected via BIC.

Regional classification: 1,713 of 1,784 wrecks classified into Western Med, Eastern Med, Adriatic, Black Sea, or Atlantic using Country, Region, and name-based heuristics.

Tree-ring data: Central European JJA temperature anomalies (Büntgen et al. 2011), sampled at 5-year intervals for visualization. Full annual data available from NOAA Paleoclimatology.

Volcanic forcing: Major eruptions from the eVolv2k v3 dataset (Toohey & Sigl 2017), showing volcanic stratospheric sulfur injection in Tg SO₂.

Numismatic data: Aureus/solidus weights compiled from Walker (ANS), OCRE, and Vrij (2016). Dirham data from Umayyad/Abbasid coinage studies and Antiquity (2023).
